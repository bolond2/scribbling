# I Am Blocked

A reference doc for the moment I sit down to write and think "I have nothing to say."
Read this before giving up on a writing session.

---

## Reframes (read these first)

- **"Having something to say" is a result of writing, not a prerequisite.** The act of explaining something is often how you discover you understand it — or find the exact gap you don't. Writing is a thinking tool, not just an output of finished thinking.
- **You don't need novel insight. You need *your* explanation.** Someone else has written about this topic. Your version, with your framing and your analogy, is still worth writing — especially since you're the one it's training.
- **"Simple" is the point, not a concession.** Explaining something ordinary clearly is harder and more valuable than sounding smart about something obscure. Almost nobody does it well.
- **The bar is "helped me think" or "helped one reader,"** not "impressed everyone."

---

## Where topics actually come from

Stop trying to think of "good topics" in the abstract. Mine these sources instead:

1. **Questions I had to answer for someone recently.** Any time in the last two weeks I explained something to a kid, coworker, or friend — that's a post. The thinking is already done; just repeat it in writing.
2. **Things I looked up and had to relearn.** If I Googled it, someone else will too. Write the explanation I wish I'd found.
3. **A decision I made and why.** Not a crisis — just "I chose X over Y, here's why" or "here's the system I use for Z." Decisions with reasoning are stories.
4. **A disagreement or "actually" moment.** Something mildly contrarian to common wisdom, even in a small way. Tension creates engagement.
5. **An analogy that clicked.** If I've ever thought "oh, it's like X" — that's often a whole post, because analogies are the actual mechanism of good explanation.
6. **Something that confused me until it didn't.** The confusion is the opening line. The clarity is the ending.

### My specific topic buckets
- **Kid questions** — anything my kids (ages 4–14) asked that I didn't answer well in the moment. Capture the question immediately, even unanswered; write the good version later.
- **Home & garden** — gardening, home improvement, organization projects. Natural "problem → options → what I chose → what I'd do differently" shape.
- **Professional, as it comes up** — a decision made, a tool that clicked, a way I explained something to a stakeholder. Don't force a quota; let these show up when genuinely fresh.

## Storytelling moves (use while drafting)

- **Open with tension or a question, not context.** "Our API got 40% slower and nobody knew why" beats "Our system uses a caching layer."
- **Concrete before abstract.** Lead with the specific example; let the general principle follow. People remember the instance and infer the rule.
- **Name the "why should I care" in the first two sentences.**
- **Follow one thread, not a list of facts.** Even a simple explainer should build toward something.

---

## The unblocking exercise

When stuck, answer this out loud or in one sentence:

> "What's one thing — technical or not — that I had to explain to someone in the last month?"

Write that sentence down. That's the post. Start drafting from there, don't wait for a better topic to appear.

---

## Process reminders

- Draft first, edit for concision second — don't do both at once.
- One-sentence test before/after drafting: *what does the reader need to know or do?*
- Short and frequent beats long and rare. 400 words, twice a week, beats 2000 words once a quarter.
- Work-specific or sensitive material: full detail stays in a private note; only the genericized version goes in the public repo.
